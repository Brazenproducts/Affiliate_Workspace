How to choose the best weighted vest

The right weighted vest depends on how you plan to use it:

  • Starting weight — Most beginners do well starting at 10-20% of their body weight. Too heavy too fast leads to compensated movement patterns and joint strain.
  • Fit and stability — A vest that shifts during movement is both ineffective and annoying. Look for adjustable straps at the chest, shoulders, and waist. The Hyperwear Hyper Vest PRO is the gold standard for running stability.
  • Use case — For walking and general fitness, a simple sand-filled vest works well. For pull-ups and bodyweight work, a slim low-profile vest like V-Force is better. For CrossFit and barbell work, use a plate carrier.
  • Adjustability — Vests like Cross101 that grow with you via add-on weight pouches are better long-term investments than fixed-weight options.
  • Breathability — For outdoor use in warm weather, look for mesh panels or breathable fabrics. Solid neoprene vests trap heat quickly.

Frequently asked questions

What weight should I start with in a weighted vest?

Start at 5-10% of your bodyweight for running and 10-20% for walking or bodyweight exercises. For a 170-pound person, that means starting around 10-17 pounds for most uses. Build up gradually as your body adapts.

Can you wear a weighted vest every day?

Yes, many people wear weighted vests during daily walks without issue. Start with shorter sessions and lighter weights. If you notice joint pain, lower back soreness, or changes to your posture or gait, take a day off and reduce the weight.

Are weighted vests good for losing weight?
Wearing a weighted vest increases caloric expenditure during any activity. Studies show even a 10-pound vest can increase calorie burn during walking by 8-12%. Combined with a consistent routine, it is a meaningful addition to a weight management program.
What is the difference between a plate carrier and a traditional weighted vest?
Plate carriers like the Rogue vest use solid weight plates inserted into front and back pouches. Traditional weighted vests use smaller iron or sand inserts distributed across the vest. Plate carriers offer a cleaner weight-to-bulk ratio and are preferred by CrossFit athletes.
